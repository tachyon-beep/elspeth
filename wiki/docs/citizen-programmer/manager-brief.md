# Brief for Managers

Your team members may be building software with AI tools without realising it. A business analyst who uses an AI tool to build a reporting dashboard, a data integration, or a workflow automation is producing executable logic that can affect your organisation's data integrity, security, and audit trails — even though they don't think of themselves as programmers, and even though they have legitimate access to the systems involved.

This is not about blame or prohibition. AI tools let domain specialists turn their knowledge into working tools faster than ever before, and the results are often genuinely useful. The risk is that these tools can affect shared systems in ways that need professional review — and right now, nobody may know they exist.

## What to Do

**Have a team conversation about AI tool usage.** Ask your team: has anyone used an AI tool to build something that connects to a shared system, runs on a schedule, or automates a workflow? The goal is visibility — knowing what exists so IT can help make it safe.

**Share the [self-assessment checklist](index.md#self-assessment) with your team.** It takes two minutes to read and helps people recognise whether something they've built might need a second look.

**Loop in IT for a lightweight review.** If team members have built tools that connect to shared databases, automate approvals, or integrate with external systems, ask IT to take a look. This doesn't need to be a formal audit — a short conversation about what the tool does, what happens when things go wrong, and whether anyone else needs to know about it is usually enough to identify whether further work is needed.

**Make it safe to speak up.** People won't tell you about things they've built if they think they'll get in trouble. Frame this as "let's make sure the useful things you've built are safe" rather than "you shouldn't have done that." The person who built a working integration that their team relies on every day has done something valuable — they just need help with the parts that are hard to get right without IT expertise.
